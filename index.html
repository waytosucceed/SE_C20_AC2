Ratio

Motivation :

Ratios are like secret recipes for comparing things! They help us understand how much of one thing we need compared to another. Imagine baking a cake: a ratio tells you how much flour to use for a certain amount of sugar. Or think about sports: a win-loss ratio tells you how well a team is doing. Learning about ratios unlocks a superpower for understanding the world around you, from cooking and sports to art and even science. It's all about comparing and understanding relationships between numbers, making it easier to make sense of the world!

Ratio :

A ratio is a way to show a relationship between two or more quantities.

For example

if you have 3 boys and 2 girls on your team, the ratio of boys to girls is written as 3:2. This means that for every 3 boys, there are 2 girls.

Note: When providing a ratio, it’s standard practice to give the simplified form.

In ratio, the first quantity of the ratio is called antecedent and the second quantity is called Consequent.

Representing a Ratio :

The ratio can be represented in three different forms such as

  • a to b
  • a:b (read as “a is to b”)
  • ab

Note: The order of the terms in a ratio is important.

Types of Ratios :

  1. Part-to-Part Ratio : Compare different parts of a group to each other.
    • Example : In a class of 5 boys and 15 girls, the part-to-part ratio of boys to girls is 1:3.
  2. Part-to-Whole Ratio: Compares one part of the group to the whole group.
    • Example : In the same class, the part-to-whole ratio of boys is 5:20, which simplifies to 1:4.

Connection between Fraction and Ratio :

Fractions and ratios are closely related, as both express a relationship between quantities. However, while a fraction represents a part of a whole, a ratio compares two or more parts to each other or to the whole.

Fractions as Ratios: Any fraction can be seen as a ratio.
For example, the fraction 34 can be expressed as the ratio 3:4, meaning 3 parts out of 4.

Ratios as Fractions: If a ratio is given as a:b, it can be directly converted to the fraction ab.
Here, 'a' represents the numerator (the top part of the fraction), and 'b' represents the denominator (the bottom part of the fraction).

Simplifying Ratios and Fractions: Just like fractions, ratios can be simplified by dividing both parts by their greatest common divisor (GCD).
For example, the ratio 6:9 simplifies to 2:3, similar to how the fraction 69 simplifies to 23.
